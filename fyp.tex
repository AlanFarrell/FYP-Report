\subsection{Data Inputs}
The main data input to the propagation of satellites in this project is Two-Line Element sets (TLEs). TLEs are a common publicly accessible format which describes Earth orbiting satellite positions. The primary source of TLE data is SpaceTrack \cite{spacetrack}}. Although SpaceTrack is the primary source many projects such as this project and other research rely on on sources such as CelesTrak which takes the data from SpaceTrack and organizes it into specific groupings\cite{celestrakTLE}. Some examples of these groupings can be seen listed in Listing \ref{list:satellite_categories}



\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Starlink}
    \item \textbf{ISS}
    \item \textbf{Earth observation satellites}
    \item \textbf{Weather satellites}
    \item \textbf{Communications satellites}
    \item \textbf{Active vs.\ debris groupings}
\end{itemize}
\label{list:satellite_categories}

While other sites exist such as N2Y0 and AMSAT , CelesTrak remains one of the most reliable and widely used platforms due to consistent updates which are synchronized with SpaceTrack and categories which help simplify data access. Celetrak updates the majority of its TLE files approximately every two hours. Celestrak's starlink data can expect multiple updates per day, making it suitable for this projects application \cite{usradioguyNewTLE}.



\subsection{Two Line Element Data}
TLEs are a standardized format which are used to describe the orbital properties of any Earth orbiting satellites. A TLE is a representation of a satellite's orbit and a specific moment in time, this is known as the epoch. Because the TLE is simple and lightweight it is widely support and the most common way to distribute orbit data for tracking and prediction.  



A TLE consists of three lines, line 0, which is optional contains the satellite name, line 1 contains information relating to satellite identification and basic orbital parameter and line 2 describes the remaining orbital elements such as the inclination angle. \subsection{Coordinate Transformations} \label{section:coordinateTransformations}
Coverage estimation first requires the satellite and ground location to be expressed in a common frame/coordinate system at a given epoch. Satellites which are propagated using SGP4 are expressed in True Equator, Mean Equinox frame (TEME), while ground sites are typically given in geodetic coordinates (latitude and longitude). The geodetic frame is tied to the Earth and rotates with it, unlike TEME which is fixed to the stars. It is therefore necessary to convert both of these into a common frame so that necessary calculations for visibility and choosing an optimal satellite can be carried out. This was done by converting the ground site and satellite to an Earth Centered Earth Fixed (ECEF) frame \cite{asteRisk2023} \cite{astropy:2022}.

\subsubsection{TEME}
TEME is the native coordinate output of SPG4 for a satellite's position is generated from TLE data. It uses Earth's instantaneous (true) equator at an epoch combined with the mean equinox which helps to average out potential short term wobbles in the Earth's rotation. TEME is non-rotating in respective to Earth's surface. This makes it so you cannot directly compare TEME positions to geodetic locations and TEME must first be converted into ECEF \cite{asteRisk2023}. 

\subsubsection{ECEF}
The ECEF frame is a rotating cartesian coordinate system whose origin is at the Earth's center of mass. Unlike TEME, ECEF rotates with the Earth, meaning a fixed point on the surface will stay constant with a given set of coordinates over a period of time. The axes are defined relative to the Earth's geometry. The Z-axis alings with the rotation axis toward the north pole, the X-axis passes through the intersection between the equator and Greenwich meridian, and the Y-axis  complete the right handed system at 90$^\circ$ east\cite{Navipedia_CTRS_2011}. This can be visualized in figure \ref{fig:ECEF}.  

\begin{figure}[h!]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\linewidth]{figures/ECEF.png}
    \caption{Diagram displaying ECEF frame \cite{Navipedia_CTRS_2011}}
    \label{fig:ECEF}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{TEME $\rightarrow$ ECEF}
As discussed in section \ref{section:coordinateTransformations}, to compare a satellite from SGP4 with a ground point, the TEME vector must be rotated by Earth's sidereal rotation. An example of this rotation can be seen in \ref{eq:teme_to_ecef}

\begin{equation}\label{eq:teme_to_ecef}
\begin{bmatrix}
x_{\mathrm{ECEF}} \\
y_{\mathrm{ECEF}} \\
z_{\mathrm{ECEF}}
\end{bmatrix}
=
\underbrace{
\begin{bmatrix}
\cos\theta & \sin\theta & 0 \\
-\sin\theta & \cos\theta & 0 \\
0 & 0 & 1
\end{bmatrix}
}_{R_3(+\theta)}
\begin{bmatrix}
x_{\mathrm{TEME}} \\
y_{\mathrm{TEME}} \\
z_{\mathrm{TEME}}
\end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}

This aligns the satellite position with a Earth fixed rotating frame. 

\subsubsection{Geodetic $\rightarrow$ ECEF}
To compare a satellite with an observer on Earth, the observer's latitude, longitude and altitude must be expressed as cartesian X, Y, Z coordinates in the ECEF frame. As the Earth is not a perfect sphere it is approximated as a WSG-84 ellipsoid. This is defined by it's semi major axis(the equatorial size), the flattening(how much the poles are compressed) and the first eccentricity (a measure of how non spherical the ellipsoid is). This can be seen in \ref{eq:WGS-84}\cite{WGS84_ECEF_Conversion}.


\begin{equation}\label{eq:WGS-84}
    e^2 = 2f - f^2.    
\end{equation}

As the Earth bulges at the equator, the distance from the center to the surface will change with latitude. This can be handled using a prime-vertical radius of curvature as seen in \ref{eq:prime_vertical_radius}\cite{WGS84_ECEF_Conversion}. 


\begin{equation}\label{eq:prime_vertical_radius}
N(\phi)
= \frac{a}{\sqrt{1 - e^{2}\,\sin^{2}\phi}}
\end{equation}

Once $N(\phi)$ has been found the observer location can be described in ECEF as seen in \ref{eq:ecef_components}\cite{WGS84_ECEF_Conversion}.

\begin{equation}\label{eq:ecef_components}
\begin{aligned}
X &= (N(\phi) + h)\,\cos\phi\,\cos\lambda ,\\[6pt]
Y &= (N(\phi) + h)\,\cos\phi\,\sin\lambda ,\\[6pt]
Z &= \bigl(N(\phi)(1 - e^{2}) + h\bigr)\,\sin\phi .
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

These equations convert the geodetic position ($\phi, \lambda, h$) and the satellite's equation into the ECEF frame. This allows for all later geometry such as line of sight checks to be carried out. 


\subsection{Satellite Visibility Model}
Determining whether a satellite can provide coverage requires evaluating the geometric line of sight(LOS) between the observer location and the satellite. Once both of these positions are expressed in ECEF, visibility can be computed using LOS vector analysis, local horizon projection and elevation angle masking.


The first step is to compute the vector from the observer to the satellite, 

\begin{equation}
    d = r_{sat} - r_{obs}.
\end{equation}


This vector expresses the direction of the satellite relative to the observers location. IF the satellite lies below the horizon it cannot be deemed visible. 

To know if the satellite appears in the sky from an observers perspective, the LOS vector is transformed into an East-North-Up (ENU) frame. This rotates the ECEF coordinates to be aligned with the observer's.

\begin{itemize}
    \item East (E) - horizontal axis pointing east
    \item North (N) - horizontal axis pointing north
    \item Up (U) - vertical axis pointing outward from Earth. 
\end{itemize}

This transformation allows for the elevation angle to be computed \cite{Navipedia_ECEF_ENU_2011}. It cal also be visualized in figure.

\begin{figure}[h!]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\linewidth]{figures/ENU.png}
    \caption{Transformation between ENU and ECEF \cite{Navipedia_ECEF_ENU_2011}}
    \label{fig:ENU}
\end{figure}

The elevation angle then describes how high above the horizon a satellite appears as seen in \ref{eq:elevation_angle}


\begin{equation}\label{eq:elevation_angle}
    \theta_{el} = arctan \left (\frac{u}{\sqrt{e^2 + n^2}}\right )
\end{equation}

The result can be interpreted as follows:
\begin{align*}
\theta_{\mathrm{el}} > 0^\circ \;&:\; \text{satellite is above the geometric horizon},\\[4pt]
\theta_{\mathrm{el}} = 0^\circ \;&:\; \text{exactly on the horizon},\\[4pt]
\theta_{\mathrm{el}} < 0^\circ \;&:\; \text{below the horizon (not visible)}.
\end{align*}


However, in reality, satellites close to the horizon may suffer from increased attenuation, obstruction from terrain and buildings, and doppler shifts. For this reason, a 10$^{\circ}$ elevation mask is applied. Only satellites which exceed this angle are deemed visible\cite{Washburn_EarthCov_2004}. This can be seen in figure \ref{fig:MaskAngle}, where $\beta$ is the masking angle.

\begin{figure}[h!]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\linewidth]{figures/MaskAngle-cap.png}
    \caption{Masking angle \cite{Washburn_EarthCov_2004}}
    \label{fig:MaskAngle}
\end{figure}

To estimate when a satellite can provide service to a ground location, just the LOS is not sufficient. Even if the satellite can be visible from an observer location, the end user must also fall into the satellite's communication beam footprint. This project uses a simplified nadir-pointing beamwidth model to determine if the satellites cap, which can be seen in \ref{fig:MaskAngle}, covers the observe's position.

\section{Implementation}
The implementation of this project consists of a Python based system which retrieves live orbital data for LEO satellites (specifically Starlink) using Celestrak and then propagates each orbit using the SGP4 model. Using these propagations, geometric visibility checks for specific locations are calculated.  The system is designed to support coverage and capacity calculations by determining which satellites are optimal for downlink access at a given period in time.  

\subsection{Data acquisition}
The first stage of implementation is acquiring up ot date orbital Parameters for the Starlink LEO constellation. TLE sets are retrieved from Celestrak. This data provides the perturbations required  to propagate the orbits using SGP4. The TLE data used in this system are taken directly from Celestrak \cite{celestrakTLE}. Downloading this data produces a text stream which has sets of three lines of text blocks, satellite name, followed by line 1 and line 2 of the TLE. The implementation of this system parses these entries for each satellite.  

Using live TLEs ensures that the system accurately represents the dynamic nature of large LEO constellations, which orbital parameters may change drastically and regularly due to any number of factors such as manual maneuvers. 

\subsection{Orbit propagation}
Once the TLEs have been retrieved and parsed in a suitable manner, each satellite's position and velocity are computed using the SGP4 algorithm. In this system each satellites state vector can be generated as seen in Listing \ref{code:sgp4}.

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python, caption={SGP4 propagation example}, label={code:sgp4}]
satellite = Satrec.twoline2rv(line1, line2)
e, r, v = satellite.sgp4(jd, fr)
\end{lstlisting}


The outputs of the \ref{code:sgp4} are as follows:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{r}: The satellite position vector $\left(x, y, z\right)$ expressed in the True Equator Mean Equinox (TEME) reference frame.
    \item \textbf{v}: The satellite velocity vector in the TEME coordinate frame.
    \item \textbf{e}: An error flag returned by the propagator, where a value of zero indicates successful propagation and non-zero values correspond to specific SGP4 error conditions.
\end{itemize}

The Julian Date (jd, fr) is computed using the current UTC timestamp to ensure a precise propagation epoch. Any satellite with a non zero error flags are discarded to avoid propagating any invalid orbital data. 


\subsection{Coordinate Transformations}
Satellite positions produced by SGP4 are given in True Equator, Mean Equinox (TEME) format. TEME remains fixed to relative to the stars not to the Earth. It uses the Earths true equatorial plane at that moment in tim, but for the x-axis it uses the mean equinox which helps smooth out some of the short term wobbles in the Earths's rotation.  

As TEME does not rotate with the Earth, but ground locations given in latitude and longitude do rotate with the Earth, for visibility and elevation calculations, both the satellite and the observer location need to be in the same rotating frame. For this reason TEME must be converted to Earth Centered Earth Fixed (ECEF) coordinate system \cite{inproceedings}. 
 
\subsubsection{TEME to ECEF}
\endL
The conversion for TEME to ECEF is as follows \ldots

\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{r}_{\text{ECEF}} = R_{3}\!\left(\theta_{\text{GMST}}\right)\,\mathbf{r}_{\text{TEME}},
\end{equation}

\noindent\textbf{Where:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item $R_{3}(\theta)$ is a rotation matrix describing a rotation about the Earth's $Z$--axis.
    \item $\theta_{\text{GMST}}$ is the Greenwich Mean Sidereal Time at the observation epoch.
\end{itemize}

This can be written explicitly as a rotation matrix as follows \ldots

\begin{equation}
R_{3}(\theta) = 
\begin{bmatrix}
\cos\theta & -\sin\theta & 0 \\
\sin\theta &  \cos\theta & 0 \\
0          &  0          & 1
\end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}

Meaning \ldots

\begin{equation}
\begin{bmatrix}
x_{\text{ECEF}} \\
y_{\text{ECEF}} \\
z_{\text{ECEF}}
\end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
\cos\theta_{\text{GMST}} & -\sin\theta_{\text{GMST}} & 0 \\
\sin\theta_{\text{GMST}} &  \cos\theta_{\text{GMST}} & 0 \\
0                        &  0                        & 1
\end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix}
x_{\text{TEME}} \\
y_{\text{TEME}} \\
z_{\text{TEME}}
\end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}


\subsubsection{Latitude Longitude to ECEF}

\endL
\newline
\noindent\textbf{Given:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Latitude: $\phi$ \, (radians)
    \item Longitude: $\lambda$ \, (radians)
    \item Altitude: $h$ \, (meters above the WGS--84 reference ellipsoid)
\end{itemize}


\noindent\textbf{WGS--84 Ellipsoid Constants:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item $a = 6378137.0 \ \text{m}$ \quad (equatorial radius)
    \item $f = \dfrac{1}{298.257223563}$ \quad (flattening)
    \item $e^{2} = 2f - f^{2}$ \quad (first eccentricity squared)
\end{itemize}

The first step is to find the distance from Earth's center to the surface at a given latitude \ldots


\begin{equation}
N(\phi) = \frac{a}{\sqrt{1 - e^{2}\sin^{2}\phi}}.
\end{equation}


Knowing this means it is now possible to compute the ECEF coordinate.


\begin{align}
X &= \left( N(\phi) + h \right)\cos\phi\cos\lambda \\[6pt]
Y &= \left( N(\phi) + h \right)\cos\phi\sin\lambda \\[6pt]
Z &= \left( N(\phi)(1 - e^{2}) + h \right)\sin\phi
\end{align}


\subsection{Visibility Logic}
Once both the ground observer and the satellites propagated position are represented in ECEF the first filter check for visibility can be applied. This is based on computing the satellite's elevation angle relative to the observers horizon. 

Given the satellite position,

\begin{equation}
     r_{\text{satellite}}^{\text{ECEF}} = (s_x, s_y, s_z),
\end{equation}



and the observer position, 


\begin{equation}
     r_{\text{observer}}^{\text{ECEF}} = (g_x, g_y, g_z),
\end{equation}

the line of sight vector (LOS) can be represented as,



\begin{equation}
     d = r_{\text{satellite}}^{\text{ECEF}} - r_{\text{observer}}^{\text{ECEF}}= (d_x, d_y, d_z),
\end{equation}

Using the observer latitude and longitude, the LOS can be converted into local horizon coordinates. This can be done following a standard rotation. \cite{Navipedia_ECEF_ENU} 

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
e &= -\sin\lambda \, d_x + \cos\lambda \, d_y, \\
n &= -\cos\lambda \sin\phi \, d_x - \sin\lambda \sin\phi \, d_y + \cos\phi \, d_z, \\
u &= \cos\lambda \cos\phi \, d_x + \sin\lambda \cos\phi \, d_y + \sin\phi \, d_z.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

Using this, the elevation angle can then be computed as the angle between the LOS vector and the local horizon \cite{Navipedia_ECEF_ENU}. 

\begin{equation}
    \theta_{\text{el}} = \arctan\left( \frac{u}{\sqrt{e^2 + n^2}} \right)
\end{equation}

Then, to filter out any satellites which are too close to the horizon or below the horizon, a mask angle of $10^{\circ}$ is applied. 


\begin{equation}
    \text{el} \geq \theta_{\text{mask}}, \qquad 
    \theta_{\text{mask}} = 10^\circ
\end{equation}
