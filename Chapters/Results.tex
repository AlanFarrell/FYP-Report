\chapter{Results} \label{chapter:5}
The three core metrics which will be compared are coverage availability (as a percentage of time), capacity throughput (Mbps) and latency (ms). 

Coverage availability determines how often the user has service, this metric is based on visibility of satellites and a high level model of the beam-width of a given satellite. This will be compared to urban and rural 5G networks. Throughput is measured in Mbps, this is averaged over a period of time and will also be compared to urban and rural 5G throughput. 

Due to computational limitations of the system on which the simulations were executed, the initial simulation windows analyzed are limited to 12-hour periods. Specifically, simulations were conducted from 08:00 to 20:00 on Thursday, 23 April 2026. All reported results represent averages over this time interval.
All TLE data used in these simulations were obtained and stored on the morning of 23 April 2026. Each simulation was subsequently run using this same TLE dataset and over the same UTC time window, regardless of the actual execution date of the simulation. The selection of this date was arbitrary.
Again it should be noted that these capacity values represent theoretical upper bounds derived using the Shannon capacity formula and do not account for contention between users, dynamic beam scheduling, or atmospheric attenuation, meaning real-world user throughput would likely be lower under peak load conditions.

It should be noted that the interpretation of the data rate results in this chapter are all derived using the Shannon Capacity formula discussed in Section \ref{section:coverageCapabilit}. Therefore, all results represent an ideal upper bound under the assumed SINR conditions. These values are a theoretical maximum data rate for an individual user under set conditions. \section{Single Link capacity check}
To help verify future results, a single numerical check on the link budget model was performed for a representative LEO satellite. The objective is to confirm that the results are physically plausible. 

\subsection{Slant Range and Geometry}
A satellite at an altitude of 550km was chosen directly overhead to represent a LEO constellation satellite. Under this geometry, which is favorable, the slant range is 
\[
d \approx 5.5 \times 10^5 \, \mathrm{m}.
\]

\subsection{Free-Space Path Loss}
Assuming a downlink frequency of 19 Ghz, the FSPL is computed using Friis equation expressed in terms of distance and carrier frequency:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:fspl}
\mathrm{FSPL(dB)} = 20 \log_{10}(d) + 20 \log_{10}(f) - 147.55,    
\end{equation}

where \(d\) is the satellite-user slant range in metres and \(f\) is the carrier frequency in hertz.

Solving (\ref{eq:fspl}) gives:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:fspl-solved}
\mathrm{FSPL(dB)} = 20 \log_{10}(5.5 \times 10^5) + 20 \log_{10}(1.9 \times 10^{10}) - 147.55 = 172.83 dB.    
\end{equation}

\subsection{Received Signal Power}
Using the transmit power and receiver gain discussed in \ref{tab:leo_link_budget_paramaters} received power is computed in (\ref{eq:receivedpower}).

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:receivedpower}
P_{received} = P_\mathrm{transmitted} + G_\mathrm{received} - \mathrm{FSPL}.
\end{equation}

(\ref{eq:receivedPower}) can be seen solved in (\ref{eq:receivedpower-solved}).
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:receivedpower-solved}
P_{received} = 46 + 33 - 173 = -94 dBW.
\end{equation}

\subsection{SINR and Capcity Estimate}
The SINR is computed as (\ref{eq:snr}).

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:snr}
    SINR = \frac{Power_{received}}{Noise + Interference}
\end{equation}

where background thermal noise by 

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:noise}
    N = kTB \cdot NF
\end{equation}

where 
\begin{itemize}
  \item $N$ : Thermal noise power (W)
  \item $k$ : Boltzmann constant ($1.38 \times 10^{-23}\,\text{J/K}$)
  \item $T$ : Absolute temperature (K)
  \item $B$ : Bandwidth (Hz)
  \item $\mathrm{NF}$ : Noise figure (linear, unitless)
\end{itemize}

Using a noise figure of 3dB, a bandwidth of 100Mhz, and noise parameters of $k = 1.38 \times 10^{-23}\,\mathrm{J/K}$ \quad and \quad $T = 290\,\mathrm{K}$ the noise power is 

\begin{equation}
N \approx 8.0 \times 10^{-13}\,\mathrm{W}
\end{equation}

Then expressing $P_{received}= -94 dBW$ in Watts,

\begin{equation}
    P_{received}(dBW) = 10log_{10}(P(W)) \rightarrow P_{received}(W) = 10^{\frac{-94}{10}} = 4 \times 10^ {-10} W
\end{equation}

The SINR can then be computed as seen in (\ref{eq:sinrSolved}) where no interfering satellites are assumed, so $Interferance = 0$.

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:sinrSolved}
    SINR = \frac{P_{received}}{Interference + Noise} = \frac{4 \times 10 ^{-10}}{0 + 8.0 \times 10^{-13}} = 500
\end{equation}


Using the value gained in (\ref{eq:sinrSolved}), (\ref{eq:shannonCapac}) can now be solved.  
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:shannonCapac}
C = B \log_2 (1 + \mathrm{SINR}) = 100 \times 10^6log_2(1+500) \approx 897 Mbps. 
\end{equation}

The value obtained form (\ref{eq:shannonCapac}) is a best-case, noise-limited, directly overhead scenario, whereas any simulation in the following sections will involve more realistic geometry and interference. This is an absolute limit of what a sample satellite may achieve under the given simulation parameters. However, this defines an upper ceilings of what can be achieved in the following simulations. 

\section{5G performance metrics}
In order to evaluate LEO satellite metrics, it is important to have a baseline performance metric, this will be in the form of known 5G standards. According to the International Telecommunication Union's global standard or International Mobile Telecommunications (ITU IMT-2020) \cite{ITU-R-M2410-2017},peak performance assumes ideal conditions and states up to 20 Gbps down-link and 10 Gbps up-link for a single user. However, in realistic network conditions with active users and scheduling constraints, the average user experienced is expected to be at least 100 Mbps on the down-link and 50 Mbps on the up-link\cite{ITU-R-M2410-2017}.

\section{StarlinkDTC - 08:00 to 20:00, 23 April 2026}
As DTC capable Starlink satellites are not publicly available, this project uses an approximation based on what information is available. There are three things which are known to the public about DTC capable satellites. 

\begin{itemize}
  \item DTC cell payloads are carried on Starlink Gen2 and v2-Mini satellites, and not legacy v1 and v1.5 satellites \cite{StarlinkV2MiniD2C}.
  \item DTC capable satellites are clustered in specific launch batches, which can be closely matched with NORAD catalog ranges \cite{StarlinkV2MiniD2C}.
  \item Gen2 and DTC satellites operate in mid-inclination shells ($\approx 43^{\circ}$ and $\approx 53^{\circ}$) \cite{SpaceflightNow_Starlink51}
\end{itemize}
Using this information an approximate filter was applied to to the Starlink TLE data. If a satellite was found to be within certain NORAD batch ranges, and or between certain inclination shells, it was considered to be likely to be capable of DTC payloads. 
Figures \ref{fig:StarlinkDTCpercerntage} and \ref{fig:StarlinkDTCMbps} start a trend which will become apparent in the next sections. While Figure \ref{fig:StarlinkDTCpercerntage} shows the coverage availability to be only a mean of $0.18\%$ of the time, Figure \ref{fig:StarlinkDTCMbps} shows that when there is coverage, the downlink capacity is quite strong, at a mean of 99.98 Mbps and a maximum theoretical throughput of 176.37 Mbps. 

Looking at Figures \ref{fig:StarlinkDTCTrackGlobal} and \ref{fig:StarlinkDTCTracksIreland} show why this is the case, there simply is not enough satellites passing over the country to provide consistent coverage. This is entirely to do with satellite orbital geometry and has nothing to do with the satellites down-link capacities.

\section{Traditional 5G Coverage in Ireland}
The figures in \ref{fig:5g_coverage} show how terrestrial 5G coverage is spread across Ireland.\cite{comreg_coverage_map}. As expected the strongest coverage is around cities like Dublin, Cork, Galway, and Limerick. By comparison there are large rural and coastal regions, particularly in the West and the South , which have poor coverage. This reflects on the reliance of terrestrial networks and dens base station deployment. Overall these figures show that where there is 5G coverage you can expect excellence performance, however, it does not current provide consistent nationwide coverage, highlight a marketplace for a complementary solution such as LEO satellite broadband. 

The coverage patterns match the population density map in Figure \ref{fig:popDensity}, where high density areas receive the strongest terrestrial service. Figures \ref{fig:5g_coverage} highlight a limitation of solely relying on terrestrial networks for rural broadband. This serves to highlight the potential of LEO constellations for broadband as a complementary solution in Ireland.
