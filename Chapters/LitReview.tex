\chapter{Literature Review}\label{chapter:2}

The growing deployment of LEO constellations has led to a substantial volume of research focused on their coverage characteristics, performance, and sustainability for broadband delivery. Much of the existing literature focuses on modeling a simulation techniques for evaluating satellite visibility, coverage availability, and achievable link capacity. 


\section{Orbit Modelling and Regional LEO Coverage}
Morgan \textit{et al.}\cite{MorganJones2019LEO} show a detailed Kepler-based model to evaluate satellite visibility and mean time of access for fixed ground locations, showing how constellation geometry can influence regional coverage availability \cite{MorganJones2019LEO}. This work helps to establish a methodology for regional LEO coverage analysis. It also shows how LEO satellite movements can be modeled assuming Kelper's two-body mechanics. This will produce a time-depended satellite trajectory which can be defined by two vectors; initial speed $\vec{\boldsymbol{v}}(0)$ and initial position $\vec{\boldsymbol{r}}(0)$ at time $t = 0$. The use of these vectors can help to create a simple model of a satellite in orbit, where the position and speed vectors $\vec{\boldsymbol{r}}(t)$ and $\vec{\boldsymbol{v}}(t)$, respect can be expressed in Earth-fixed coordinates. The vectors $\vec{\boldsymbol{v}}(0)$ and $\vec{\boldsymbol{r}}(0)$ can also be used to calculate the elements of Kelper orbit, where each element describes a specific geometric or positional property of the orbit, as summarized below:
\begin{itemize}
  \item The \textbf{semi-major axis} defines the overall size of the orbit.
  \item The \textbf{eccentricity} describes the shape of the orbit, indicating how circular or elongated it is.
  \item The \textbf{inclination} specifies the tilt of the orbital plane relative to the Earth's equatorial plane.
  \item The \textbf{right ascension of the ascending node (RAAN)} determines the orientation of the orbital plane around the Earth's rotation axis.
  \item The \textbf{argument of perigee} defines the orientation of the orbit within its own plane.
  \item The \textbf{true anomaly} indicates the satellite's instantaneous position along the orbit.
\end{itemize}
Together, these six parameters provide a complete of the satellite's orbit in space. This means that all LEO satellites can be fully modeled using either polar coordinates with vectors $\vec{\boldsymbol{v}}$ and $\vec{\boldsymbol{r}}$ or with the six orbital elements.

Another key contribution of Morgan \textit{et al.}\cite{MorganJones2019LEO} is the link between orbital geometry and regional service availability. using Earth-centered inertial (ECI) and Earth-centered Earth-fixed (ECEF)coordinate transformations, the paper shows satellite orbital paths projected onto the Earth's surface and how coverage footprints change over time as satellites move through their orbit, this can be seen in Figure \ref{fig:SatelliteOrbitalPath}.

In order to quantify coverage performance, Morgan \textit{et al.}\cite{MorganJones2019LEO} discuss the Mean Time Satellite Visibility (MTSV) metric, which measures the long term average that a satellite remains visible from a fixed ground station. As seen in Figure \ref{fig:MSTV}, increasing the minimum elevation angle required for a satellite to be considered visible reduces both the average time the satellite can be accessed and the likelihood of long visibility periods. The upper plot illustrates that as the elevation constraint becomes more strict, the mean satellite visibility time decreases because the satellite spends less time high enough above the horizon. The lower plot further shows that when a higher elevation requirement is applied, long visibility durations become increasingly unlikely. Overall, the figure demonstrates that stricter elevation constraints lead to reduced and less reliable satellite access over a given region.

Morgan \textit{et al.}\cite{MorganJones2019LEO} also highlights that increasing constellation size does not linearly improve visibility as overlap between satellites leads to diminishing returns beyond a certain deployment density. 

While the framework presented by Morgan \textit{et al.}\cite{MorganJones2019LEO} focuses primarily on satellite visibility rather than throughput or link capacity,this study provides a solid foundation for further methodology and testing. In the context of this project, the modeling approach are directly applicable to evaluating LEO constellation coverage over Ireland. By extending this visibility framework to include coverage time and link budget, it becomes possible to move beyond service availability and quantitatively assess whether LEO satellite systems can deliver broadband performance comparable to rural 5G networks \cite{MorganJones2019LEO}.

\section{LEO Satellite Systems for Rural Connectivity}
En Salem \textit{et al.} \cite{EnSalemXXXX} extend geometric coverage analysis by including communication performance into their simulation. Instead of assuming that satellites and base stations are at fixed, exact locations, their model treats these nodes as being randomly spread out in space. This makes the simulation more realistic and helps capture how real satellite and ground networks behave on average rather than in one specific layout. Monte-Carlo simulations were ran to see how well the network performs. These simulations measured performance metrics such as coverage probability, SINR, and achievable data rate, under varying satellite and terrestrial deployment densities.

A key point of En Salem \textit{et al.} \cite{EnSalemXXXX} is the evaluation of hybrid terrestrial-LEO networks, in which satellite links operate as a complementary access layer in areas with sparse terrestrial infrastructure.En Salem \textit{et al.} \cite{EnSalemXXXX} shows that, in low-density rural areas, the addition of LEO satellites leads to an increase in coverage probability and average user throughput compared to terrestrial only deployments \cite{EnSalemXXXX}. The trends from the simulated results that compare coverage provability and rate distributions across different networks show that LEO constellations are most effective when terrestrial base station density falls below a critical threshold. Figure \ref{fig:ContinuouslyConnectedUsers} demonstrates how as As the number of simultaneous users increases, coverage probability drops, because users start to interfere with each other and network resources are shared. Systems with more base-station antennas maintain higher coverage, since they can serve multiple users more effectively. The hybrid terrestrial-satellite system consistently performs better than the terrestrial-only system, especially when the network becomes congested.

\section{Starlink Performance}
Mohan \textit{et al.} \cite{Mohan2023Starlink} presents a comprehensive evaluation of LEO satellite broadband. It analyses over 19 million crowd-sourced speed-test measurements from Starlink suers across more than 30 countries. Mohan \textit{et al.} \cite{Mohan2023Starlink} examines key performance indicators include round-trip latency, down-link and up-link throughput, and temporal variation. This allows comparison of LEO satellite broadband and terrestrial technologies such as 5G. The results show that Starlink consistently achieves median latencies below 100ms, which places Starlink LEO performance within the range of terrestrial broadband for many regions. Figure \ref{fig:ISPvsStarlinkMap} demonstrates this.

In terms of throughput, Mohan \textit{et al.} \cite{Mohan2023Starlink} reports a median down-link comparable to mid-band 5G, as seen in Figure \ref{fig:StarLinkUpDown} reporting that most Starlink Clients achieve$\approx$ 50--100 Mbps download and $\approx$ 4--12Mbps upload rates at the 75th percentile. However, in this paper it is noted that there is significant variability in both latency and throughput which they attribute to hand-overs, dynamic beam allocation, and changing network conditions as opposed to limitation of the user's equipment. This shows that user-experienced data rates are strongly influenced by constellation geometry and network dynamics.

\section{Starlink Capacity Analysis}
Meinrath \textit{et al.} \cite{XLabStarlink2025} reviews the scaling and capacity limits of LEO satellite broadband through analytic and heuristic modeling od Starlink. Rather than measuring user experienced throughput, their work is based on the theoretical capacity limited imposed by satellite beam width, spatial reuse, and user density within a beam footprint. By modeling how a total beam capacity is shared among active users, the authors show that achievable per-user data rate decreases rapidly as the number of simultaneous users increases. 

One key finding discussed by Meinrath \textit{et al.} \cite{XLabStarlink2025} is that LEO satellite performance is greatly limited by the number of users sharing the same satellite beam at one time. Using Starlink V2 capacity assumptions, the study estimates a per-beam down-link capacity of approximately 5Gbps and an uplink capacity of approximately 0.4Gbps, which must be shared among all active users within the beam foot print. Under whatMeinrath \textit{et al.} \cite{XLabStarlink2025} states to be a typical 20:1 contention ratio, which is approximately 400-420 users per beam, this translates to a maximum user density of 2.3-2.7 households per $km^2$ for a beam which covers approximately 100 $km^2$. Beyond this threshold, per-user throughput falls rapidly. 

Meinrath \textit{et al.} \cite{XLabStarlink2025} distinguishes between instantaneous peak performance and long term capacity. While individual users may experience short term peak performance and speeds exceeding 100 Mbps while beam contention is low, the report states that maintaining these rates at scale is not feasible unless constellation density also increases.

\section{Gap in Literature}
Existing literature demonstrates that LEO constellations are capable of delivering broad-band grade performance in practice and provide valuable insight into both real-world performance and theoretical capacity limits. Some studies evaluate that operational systems like Starlink can achieve low latency and competitive data rates under real usage conditions, while analytical works like Meinrath \textit{et al.} \cite{XLabStarlink2025} establish upper bounds on per-user capacity based on beam resource constraints. However these studies are measurement driven and do not directly evaluate performance at a national scale using orbit modeling. Prior simulation based research on LEO systems has largely focus on global coverage without integrating country specific contexts. In particular there is limited work that combines Kelper-based orbital simulation, visibility analysis, and theoretical link budget estimations to assess whether LEO constellations can provide broadband comparable with rural 5G within a specific country. 

As a result of this, a gap exists in the literature for an Ireland-specific, simulation driven evaluation of LEO satellite broadband that quantifies both coverage availability and achievable link budget using real orbital data. THis project addresses this gap by developing a reproducible simulation framework which models LEO satellite visibility and theoretical capacity over Ireland, enabling a direct comparison with terrestrial 5G performance and assessment of LEO systems as both a standalone system and a complementary broadband solution.
