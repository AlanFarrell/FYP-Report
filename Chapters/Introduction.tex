\chapter{Introduction} \label{chapter:1}
Reliable broadband has become fundamental for modern life, enabling access to information, communication, education, and economic opportunities. However, many rural areas in Ireland still lack access to high-speed broadband connectivity, creating a significant digital divide between urban and rural communities. This disparity limits the ability of rural residents to fully participate in the digital economy and access essential online services.

In Ireland, this divide can be particularly pronounced, despite initiatives such as the NationaL BroadBand Plan (NBBP) which aims to provide high-speed broadband to all premises by 2028. Large Geographical areas with low population density, such as the west of Ireland, present significant challenges for traditional terrestrial broadband deployment due to high infrastructure costs and logistical difficulties. As a result, many rural communities remain under-served, with limited options for reliable internet access. As a result, technologies capable of delivering high-speed, low-latency, broadband without extensive ground infrastructure should be explored. 

Low Earth Orbit (LEO) satellite systems have emerged as a potential solution to these challenges. Operating at altitudes significantly lower than geostationary satellites, LEO constellations can offer reduced latency, improved link budgets and more flexible coverage. This project aims to evaluate the potential of LEO satellite systems to provide broadband connectivity to rural areas in Ireland. By modelling the coverage and performance of existing and planned LEO constellations, this project seeks to assess their ability to bridge the digital divide and provide reliable internet access to under-served communities.

This project will address these challenges by developing a simulation to evaluate the coverage and capacity potential of LEO satellite systems over Ireland, using publicly available orbital data and established propagation models, the work will quantify satellite coverage times, and theoretical data rates across a grid of locations in Ireland. The results will provide insights into the feasibility of LEO satellite systems as a solution for rural broadband connectivity.

\section{What is LEO}
LEO satellites orbit closely to the Earth's surface, this can be anywhere below $\approx 1500\,\text{km}$, however some even orbit as low as 160km. These satellites do not have to follow a particular orbit and are not locked in one spot above the Earth. LEO satellites have a multitude of uses such as satellite imaging of the Earth due to the distance of orbit they are able to take images at a higher resolution. The Internation space station (ISS) is also in low earth orbit, this means it is cheaper and easier for the astronauts to travel to and from it. This report is largely concerned with LEOs ability to send data quickly back to Earth due to it's close proximity. Often times these satellites work as part of a constellation in order to achieve constant coverage over an area\cite{esaLEOImage2020}.

This is slightly different from other types of satellites such as Geostationary orbit (GEO), medium Earth orbit satellites (MEO). GEO satellites match Earth's rotation, taking a full 23 hours and 56 minutes to orbit. These satellites are locked into place at a fixed orbit, traveling around 3 kilometers per second at an altitude of 35786 Km. GEO satellites are often used as telecommunication satellites, allowing antennas on the Earth to remain at one location. This effect is strengthened by the effect that one satellite can service a significant portion of the Earth's surface, needing only three satellites to achieve nearly global coverage. The distance from which these satellites orbit is what makes them useful for certain telecommunications and as weather satellites, but this can also be a drawback, due to the enormous distance between a satellite and a user, communications can be slow.\cite{esaOrbits} 

MEO satellites can cover a large range of altitudes and occupy the space between LEO and GEO. MEO satellites are largely used for navigation such as GPS on your phone. One example of this is the Galileo constellation. Again these satellites can also suffer from slow communications due to long round trips the data has to make. \cite{esaOrbits}

\section{Problem Statement}
Despite investment in Ireland's terrestrial broadband infrastructure through initiatives such as the National Broadband Plan \cite{NBPPolicy}, substantial challenges remain in delivering consistent high-preforming broadband connectivity to rural and sparsely populated ares. The  Economic and technical limitations with fibre deployment and mobile network density results in long rollout times with uneven service availability. While LEO constellations have emerged as a potential alternative capable of providing coverage for large areas with reduced dependency on ground based infrastructure, their practical suitability for addressing Ireland's rural connectivity challenges should be sufficiently quantified to measure how much these constellations could either provide their own service or complement an existing network. 
\section{Research Objectives}
The key objective of this project is to evaluate if LEO satellite constellations are capable of providing a comparable or improved broadband service to 5G across rural Ireland which may be under-served under the NBP \cite{NBPMap}. A secondary goal is to assess whether LEO systems are suitable to be part of a complementary solution to existing broadband networks. In order to achieve this a simulation framework for modelling must be developed which is capable of estimate the theoretical capacity achievable by LEO satellite links.

\section{Scope of Project}
The scope of this project is focused on evaluating the feasibility of LEO satellite constellations as a solution for rural broadband connectivity in Ireland. The work concentrates on modelling and analyzing satellite coverage availability and theoretical communication performance using a simulation based approached with publicly available data. 

This project includes the development of a software simulation for LEO satellites using real Two-Line Element (TLE) data. The simulation will evaluate satellite visibility, coverage, and theoretical data rates across a grid of locations in Ireland. The project will compare the performance of LEO satellite systems against terrestrial broadband solutions in terms of coverage and potential data rates.

The geographic scope is limited to Ireland, with a particular emphasis on rural areas that are currently under-served by terrestrial broadband. The project will focus on existing and planned LEO constellations, such as Starlink, OneWeb, and Kuiper, to assess their potential impact on rural connectivity in Ireland.

Several aspects are intentionally excluded from the scope of this project. The simulation does not account for detail processes such as modulation schemes,  encoding, adaptive power control or access scheduling. Atmospheric effects, terrain obstructions, attenuation from weather, and traffic from user demand are not explicitly considered. On top of this, network level behaviors such as gateway placement, routing strategies, handover delays,  and are not within the scope of this project. 

By constraining the scope, this project aims to deliver a clear, reproducible, any computationally achievable assessment of whether LEO satellite systems can meaningfully enhance broadband connectivity in rural Ireland.
\section{Thesis Structure}
This thesis is organized into six chapters. 

Chapter \ref{chapter:1} introduces the motivation for the study and outlines the challenges of broadband connectivity in rural Ireland, and the potential of LEO satellite systems as a solution. It presents the problem context, research objectives, scope of the work and the structure of the thesis.

Chapter \ref{chapter:2} reviews literature on LEO satellite communication systems, rural broadband connectivity, non-terrestrial networks, and LEO coverage in other countries. 

Chapter \ref{chapter:3} describes the system design and methodology used in the this project. This chapter details the modelling approach, data sources, orbit propagation, coordinate systems, and the assumptions which are used throughout the project.

Chapter \ref{chapter:4} describes the software implementation of the project. The simulation architecture, data processing workflow, coordinate transformation logic, visibility and coverage checks, performance metrics, optimization of the project and computational complexity are all discussed in this chapter.

Chapter \ref{chapter:5} presents the results of the simulations, including coverage maps, availability statistics, and theoretical throughput estimates for the evaluated LEO constellations, and comparative performance against terrestrial broadband solutions. 

Finally, Chapter \ref{chapter:6} concludes the thesis by summarizing the key findings, discussing their implications for rural broadband connectivity, and suggesting directions for future research in this area. This chapter will also reflect on the limitations of the study and potential improvements to the simulation model.
